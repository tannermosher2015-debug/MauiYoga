About · Christina Martini

Two disciplines.
One pair of hands.

For thirty years she has kept people safe in two places at once — the Ashtanga mat and the hospital ward. This is how those paths became one practice.

Three decades ago, before any of this was a school, Christina was a patient. She faced early-stage cancer and healed the long way — breath, movement, prayer, meditation, and nutrition woven into a disciplined daily routine, alongside her medical care. She has practiced every day since.

She came to Ashtanga the traditional way — daily, in person, under teachers who learned directly from Sri K. Pattabhi Jois. She studied with Manju Jois, Nancy Gilgoff and David Williams, and with Ken Harakuma, Jo Ichihara, Maty Ezraty, Sharath Jois and Saraswathi Jois. That lineage isn’t a logo on a certificate; it’s a way of teaching she still keeps.

Alongside it she built a career in medicine — a licensed oncology and chronic-care nurse and patient advocate, at the side of people facing the hardest stretches of their lives. Somewhere along the way the two stopped being separate. The breath that steadies a frightened patient is the breath that opens a posture; the care that protects a healing joint is the patience Ashtanga has always asked for. Today she teaches one integrated practice — ancient form, clinical care — at Shala Santosha in upcountry Maui.

Where she stands

God first. The door open to all.

Christina’s foundation is her Christian faith. It’s where her sense of service and stewardship begins, she keeps it first in her life, and she doesn’t hide it.

She’s also clear about a distinction: she treats yoga as a discipline for health, healing, and honest effort — a way of caring for the body God entrusted to her — not as her faith. The shala asks nothing of your beliefs. Beginners, skeptics, the injured, the elderly, the unwell: the door is the same width for all of you.

“Take active responsibility for your own health — the mat is just where we practice it.”
Christina Martini
