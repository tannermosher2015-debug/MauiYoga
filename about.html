About · Christina Martini

Two disciplines.
One pair of hands.

For thirty years she has kept people safe in two places at once — the Ashtanga mat and the hospital ward. This is how those paths became one practice.

Christina, Maui Candid vertical 3:4

Christina came to Ashtanga the traditional way — daily, in person, under teachers who learned directly from Sri K. Pattabhi Jois. She studied with Manju Jois and trained alongside Nancy Gilgoff and David Williams, two of the first Westerners to bring the practice home. That lineage isn’t a logo on a certificate. It’s a way of teaching she still keeps.

At the same time, she built a career in medicine — as an oncology and chronic-care nurse and patient advocate, at the side of people facing the hardest stretches of their lives. She watched what bodies endure, and what gives them back a sense of control.

Somewhere along the way the two stopped being separate. The breath that steadies a nervous patient is the breath that opens a posture. The careful progression that protects a healing joint is the same patience Ashtanga has always asked for. Today she teaches one integrated practice — ancient form, clinical care — at Shala Santosha in upcountry Maui.

Where she stands

Rooted in faith. Open to everyone.

Christina’s own grounding is Christian. It’s where her sense of service and stewardship comes from, and she doesn’t hide it. But the shala asks nothing of your beliefs.

She treats yoga as a discipline for health, healing, and honest effort — a practice that meets you wherever you are, whatever you hold sacred or don’t. Beginners, skeptics, the injured, the elderly, the unwell: the door is the same width for all of you.
